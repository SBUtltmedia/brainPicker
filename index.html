About MRI

Scientists and Clinicians can distinguish parts of the central nervous system both by function and appearance, or morphology. In this game, you will identify different regions in the brain in an MRI scan.

MRI, or magnetic resonance imaging, is a technique used to inspect the brain. MRI data consists of a series of images or “slices”.

In MRI slices such as these, areas containing mostly hydrophilic molecules, for example the ventricles containing CSF, appear dark. Areas containing large amounts of hydrophobic molecules, such as the regions containing many myelinated axons, appear white. Areas containing lots of cell bodies appear to be gray.

Use this game to help you practice locating the different regions of the brain. You will move through axial slices of the brain, from the top of the head down to the base of the brain and the top of the spinal cord.

Anatomical Axes

Scientists use the anatomical axes to describe the locations of brain structures the way cardinal directions are used to describe geographical locations. The cerebellum for example, is posterior to the brainstem in the same way Europe is north of Africa.

To the right is a concise diagram of the anatomical axes used in this game. A complete list can be viewed here. Note that the rostral-caudal axis changes orientation after the brainstem ends.

Anatomical Planes

From Wikipedia: source

  • An axial (also known as transverse or horizontal) plane is an X-Z plane, parallel to the ground, which (in humans) separates the superior from the inferior, or put another way, the head from the feet.
  • A coronal (also known as frontal) plane is a Y-X plane, perpendicular to the ground, which (in humans) separates the anterior from the posterior, the front from the back, the ventral from the dorsal.
  • A sagittal (also known as lateral) plane is an Y-Z plane, perpendicular to the ground, which separates left from right. The midsagittal plane is the specific sagittal plane that is exactly in the middle of the body.
